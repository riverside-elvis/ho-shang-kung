# Ch. 15 How to display Te.

Those of yore who ably were masters

> <div class="commentary">This means the superior men who attained Tao.</div>

>> <div class="note">Li Ch'iao thinks, as well as Ma Hsü-lun (Lao-tse ho-ku I, 75a/b) and Kao Heng (Lao-tse cheng-ku I, 18a/b), that Ho-shang-kung's text originally had the wording 善為道者 "those who ably fulfilled Tao" instead of 善為士者, as a quotation in the commentary of the Hou Han-shu 97, 6b (Tang-ku-chuan) shows. The same may be gathered from an allusion in Wen-tse 3, 2a which runs thus: 古之為道者理情 etc.</div>

[where] subtle and mysterious, [thus] penetrating the dark one.

> <div class="commentary">The dark one is heaven. This means: Their will was the mysterious essence of the dark one by heavenly penetration.</div>

>> <div class="note">On the explanation of hsüan as "heaven" see note on ch. 1.</div>

The depth cannot be fathomed.

> <div class="commentary">Tao and Te are deep and wide. They cannot be understood and known. Inner sight is like blind, listening back is like deaf. Nobody knows what endures.</div>

Now as it cannot be fathomed, therefore I endeavour to make it accessible.

> <div class="commentary">These permutations of unity are expressed in the following sentence.</div>

>> <div class="note">V.1.: This is expressed in the following sentence.  
---The meaning of 容 jung, here tentatively translated by "accessible" is obscure, and that Ho-shang-kung does not try to explain it seems to show that he was at a loss to devise a plausible explanation, which is the more remarkable as he is generally very ready with arbitrary interpretations of difficult expressions. Probably jung is a word from the Ch'u dialect the meaning of which is lost.</div>

Cautious like crossing a stream in winter.

> <div class="commentary">In difficult undertakings one ought to be cautious, o, like a man who crosses a stream in winter. Within one's heart one ought to think it difficult.</div>

Suspicious like afraid of the four neighbours.

> <div class="commentary">Such as these approach quite full of fear, as if they adhered to a rule, like somebody who transgresses the law and is afraid of his four neighbours whom he knows.</div>

Reticent like a guest.

> <div class="commentary">Like a guest who stands in awe of the master of the house, thus being reticent without doing anything.</div>

Dissolving like ice that is going to melt.

> <div class="commentary">What dissolves, breaks up. What melts, vanishes. By doing away with the feelings and getting rid of the desires, one daily becomes more empty.</div>

Simple like unworked wood.

> <div class="commentary">What is simple is material and firm. The form of unworked wood is not yet carved. Within one ought to take care of the spirits, outwards one ought not to be pretentious.</div>

Empty like a valley.

> <div class="commentary">Emptiness is wide and vast. A valley is empty, without Te and merit and fame, without a place. One does not grasp its existence.</div>

>> <div class="note">Instead of 不包有 "one does not grasp its existence" the Tao-tsang edition reads | | 也 "one does not grasp it". Li Ch'iao remarks that in ch. 26 of the Hus-yen-ching yin-i the first sentence of the commentary is quoted as 曠廣大也. The meaning remains the same.</div>

Muddy, o, like turbid water.

> <div class="commentary">Muddiness keeps its original purity. Turbid water is not so bright. One ought to unite with the crowd and not to keep apart.</div>

Turbid water by being quieted gradually becomes clear.

> <div class="commentary">To quiet means to bring to a standstill. If water is turbid, one should bring it to a standstill and quiet it, and gradually it will become clear of itself.</div>

>> <div class="note">Text and commentary as given by Li Ch'iao. The ordinary reading of the text is: "Who is able gradually to clear the turbid by quieting it" (Tao-tsang edition: "by bringing it to a standstill and quieting it"). The accompanying commentary runs thus: "Who is able to find out how the turbid state of the water is brought to a standstill and quieted?"</div>

The quiet by moving it gradually becomes quickened.

> <div class="commentary">Who is able to quicken the quiet slowly and gradually?</div>

>> <div class="note">In the Tao-tsang edition, the text of Lao-tse runs parallel to the foregoing line: "Who is able to move the quiet by gradually quickening it?"</div>

Who keeps this Tao does not want fulness.

> <div class="commentary">Who keeps this Tao of gradual living wants no luxurious fulness.</div>

Now who is without fulness, is thereby able to become worn out without being newly mended.

> <div class="commentary">Who is now a man without fulness is able to keep his outwornness without being in need of new mending. Who is outworn hides the splendour, who is newly mended honours merit and glory.</div>
