# Ch. 4 What has no origin.

Tao is within, and if it is used,

> <div class="commentary">ch'ung 沖 = chung 中 within. Tao hides its name and conceals its praise. Its use is within. In one school it is said: Tao becomes useful by being harmonious within. Therefore it is said "within".</div>

>> <div class="note">The provenance of this quotation is not to be ascertained.</div>

it is always not full.

> <div class="commentary">或 = 常 always. Tao is always modest and not conceited.</div>

An abyss, o! like the ancestor of all things.

> <div class="commentary">Tao is deep like an abyss and unknowable, as if it were the ancestor of all things.</div>

Stop these approaches.

> <div class="commentary">挫 = 止 to stop. 銳 = 進 to approach. Man wishes to approach the essence and to acquire merit and glory. He ought to stop this. Law and Tao are themselves not visible.</div>

>> <div class="note">Instead of 精 "essence" v.1. 情 "feelings". Li Ch'iao puts this variant into the text, though it gives a less good meaning. Another variant 愼 "cautiousness, sincerity" is doubtless to be rejected. Instead of 自見 "themselves visible" there are variants 同 "identical" and 自見同 "themselves visibly identical". Law (fa), appearing in the last sentence before Tao, might be its Buddhist equivalent Dharma and so conceivably point to Buddhist influence.  
--- Compare the largely varying explanations given in ch. 56 of this sentence and the three next ones.</div>

Loosen these connections.

> <div class="commentary">紛 = bound to hatred. Remember Tao and non-action. Thereby they will be loosened.</div>

>> <div class="note">Instead of 恨 "hatred" v.1. 根 "root", rightly rejected by Li Ch'iao.</div>

Harmonize this splendour.

> <div class="commentary">This means: Though you have the light of unique insight, you ought to know darkness and not to irritate others by your splendour.</div>

Become one with this dust.

> <div class="commentary">You ought to side with the masses, to unite yourself with dust and not to keep aloof.</div>

Still, o! as if enduring.

> <div class="commentary">This means that one ought to remain quite still and peaceful. Then one is able to endure long without perishing.</div>

I do not know whose child it is.

> <div class="commentary">Lao-tse says: I do not know where Tao came from.</div>

It seems to have preceded Ti.

> <div class="commentary">Tao was even there before the God of heaven. This means: Tao thus preceded the birth of the God of heaven. That it exists down to present times, is because it is able to be peaceful, quiet and still without troubling. It wants to induce man to perfect himself and to take Tao for his model.</div>

>> <div class="note">Ho-shang-kung seems to allude to an otherwise unknown myth of the birth of Shang-ti (from a primeval goddess? Tao itself?) But a variant not given by Li Ch'iao but contained in the Tao-tsang edition runs thus: 道乃先天地生 "Tao thus preceded the birth of heaven and earth" which would merely be an allusion to ch. 25.</div>
