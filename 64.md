# Ch. 64 How to keep the mystery.

What is at rest is easily held fast.

> <div class="commentary">The practice of asceticism and the government of the country are easy to keep for the restful one.</div>

What is not yet prognosticated is easily planned.

> <div class="commentary">At the time when the misfortune of desire has not yet taken form, it is easily brought to a standstill.</div>

What is weak is easily broken.

> <div class="commentary">Misfortune has not yet sprouted from its germs, desire has not yet become visible in the appearance. If something is weak, it is easily broken.</div>

What is subtle is easily scattered.

> <div class="commentary">What has not yet come to light, being subtle and small, is easily dispersed.</div>

>> <div class="note">V.1.: "such affairs as have not yet etc."</div>

This is the relation of creation to the not yet existing,

> <div class="commentary">If one wants to create something, it must not yet exist. At the time when something sprouts, one ought to impede its beginning.</div>

>> <div class="note">Instead of 當於 "must" v.1. | 以, same meaning.</div>

the relation of domination to the not yet confused.

> <div class="commentary">Practise asceticism and dominate the country when confusion has not yet arisen. One must shut the gate to it beforehand.</div>

A tree [which can only be] embraced by several men grows from a tiny bit.

> <div class="commentary">Proceeding from the small one completes the great.</div>

A tower of nine storeys rises out of heaped-up earth.

> <div class="commentary">Proceeding from the low one reaches the high.</div>

A journey of a thousand miles begins with putting down one foot.

> <div class="commentary">Proceeding from what is near one reaches what is far away.</div>

Who occupies himself with it spoils it.

> <div class="commentary">Who occupies himself with affairs renounces his natural state. Who occupies himself with justice renounces benignity and humanity. Who occupies himself with a beauty renounces the spirit.</div>

Who grasps it, loses it.

> <div class="commentary">Who grasps for gain, meets with misfortune. Who grasps the enemy, loses the body. What is firmly grasped, is not acquired. What is refused, returns.</div>

>> <div class="note">Comp. the explanations of this and the following sentences in ch. 29.</div>

This is why the saint does nothing. Therefore he spoils nothing.

> <div class="commentary">The saint does not trouble about the exterior, nor about gain and beauties, nor about hurtful things. Therefore nothing is spoilt.</div>

>> <div class="note">Instead of 害 "hurtful things" v.1. 敗 "robbers", which makes no sense. Instead of 壞 "spoilt" v.1. | 賊, same meaning.</div>

Because he grasps nothing, he loses nothing.

> <div class="commentary">The saint has got Te to instruct the stupid. He has got riches to help the poor. There is nothing which he should retain. Therefore he has nothing to lose to others.</div>

When the people are doing business, they always complete little and spoil it.

> <div class="commentary">從 (lit. to follow) = 爲 to do. If somebody is doing business, by his merit Te is always somewhat completed, but he covets a position, fame, greatness and fulness, and thus spoils it.</div>

By being cautious to the end as well as in the beginning, one does not spoil one's affairs.

> <div class="commentary">One ought to be in the end as in the beginning. One must not become negligent.</div>

Therefore the saint desires the undesirable.

> <div class="commentary">The saint desires what others do not desire. Men desire to be brilliant. The saint desires hidden splendour. Men desire outward show. The saint desires reality and simplicity. Men desire beauty. The saint desires Te.</div>

>> <div class="note">Instead of □ "hidden" v.1. 伏 rejected by Li Ch'iao, though the meaning is the same.</div>

He does not esteem riches difficult to obtain.

> <div class="commentary">The saint does not dazzle by wearing ornaments. He does not disdain the stone, nor does he esteem the jade.</div>

>> <div class="note">See last note on ch. 39.</div>

He learns what cannot be learned.

> <div class="commentary">The saint learns what others are unable to learn. Men learn wisdom and hypocrisy; the saint learns naturalness. Men learn to govern the world; the saint learns to practise asceticism and to guard the wisdom of Tao.</div>

He reverses what all others pass by.

> <div class="commentary">All men learn the contrary of what is asked for. They pass by the root and work with the top; they pass by the fruit and work with the flower. Who reverses this causes the return to the root.</div>

Thereby he helps all beings to become natural.

> <div class="commentary">Who teaches men to return to the root and to the fruit, thereby wants to help all beings towards the natural state of their character.</div>

But he does not dare to act.

> <div class="commentary">The saint works inactively. He does not dare to create anything. He is afraid to remove the root.</div>

>> <div class="note">Instead of 遠 "to remove" v.1. 離 "to cut off".</div>
