# Ch. 13 How to loathe shame.

Inclination and disinclination are like fear.

> <div class="commentary">Bodily inclination is like fear, and bodily disinclination is also like fear.</div>

One is afraid that great anxiety might reach the body.

> <div class="commentary">貴 kuei (honour) = 畏 wei to be afraid. 若 jo (like) = 至 che to reach. One is afraid of great anxiety that may reach the body. Therefore there is fear.</div>

>> <div class="note">V.1.: Therefore everything is fear.  
--- It need hardly be said that the explanations of kuei = wei and of jo = che are entirely arbitrary and without any linguistic or material foundation.</div>

What means inclination and disinclination?

> <div class="commentary">He asks wherein inclination and disinclination consist. Inclination is honour. Disinclination is shame touching the body. By once more asking himself he enlightens others.</div>

>> <div class="note">Instead of ju "shame" one edition has 補 pu "help", evidently a mere misprint.</div>

Inclination effects humiliation.

> <div class="commentary">Inclination and disinclination effect humiliation and slightening.</div>

>> <div class="note">After Li Ch'iao, ch'ung "inclination" is missing in the Ching-lun-t'ang edition. If this reading is correct, the original text of Ho-shang-kung must have run ju wei hsia "disinclination effects humiliation". The text as given by Li Ch'iao must be wrong at all events, the current reading presupposing the wording ch'ung ju wei hsia "inclination and disinclination effect humiliation". In the Tao-tsang edition, the text of Lao-tse runs "disinclination effects humiliation" and that of Ho-shang-kung "disinclination effects humiliation and slightening". Comp. Ma Hsü-lun, Lao-tse ho-ku I, 67a.</div>

To win it is like fear.

> <div class="commentary">To win honours is the state of fear. To be in an exalted position is like approaching deep danger. Who is honoured should not dare to be haughty. Who is rich should not dare to be a spendthrift.</div>

To lose it is like fear.

> <div class="commentary">To lose means to lose inclination and to endure disinclination. Fear means the dread of the return of calamity.</div>

This means that inclination and disinclination are like fear.

> <div class="commentary">He explains the foregoing: To win it is like fear; to lose it is like fear.</div>

What means: One is afraid that great anxiety might touch the body?

> <div class="commentary">He returns once more to his question why it is to be feared that great anxiety might touch the body.</div>

>> <div class="note">The Tao-tsang edition has only: He returns once more to his question.</div>

That I have great anxiety is because I have got a body.

> <div class="commentary">That I have great anxiety because I have got a body [means]: Who has got a body, is anxious about its hardships and thinks of it as being hungry and cold. If one hits against feelings and causes desires to follow, one is sure to meet with calamity.</div>

And if I have no body, what anxiety have I got?

> <div class="commentary">If I am allowed to be without a body, then it is natural for me to reach Tao. If I lightly soar, rising to the clouds, wander to and from where there is no space, penetrate the spirits with Tao, what anxiety could I possibly have?</div>

Therefore if one esteems the body when governing the empire, one may temporarily be entrusted with the empire.

> <div class="commentary">This means: If a prince who esteems his own personality and despises the people wants to become ruler of the empire, he may be temporarily enthroned but will not last.</div>

If one loves the body when governing the empire, one may be constantly entrusted with the empire.

> <div class="commentary">This means: If a prince is able to love his personality without caring for himself, then he wants to become father and mother of the people. By achieving this he will become ruler of the empire. Then he may entrust his person to the elders of the people without making mistakes.</div>
