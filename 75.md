# Ch. 75 How to diminish craving.

The hunger of the people comes from their prince's consuming the bulk of the revenues.

> <div class="commentary">That the people are hungry and cold has its reason in their prince's raising too many taxes from his subjects.</div>

Therefore they are hungry.

> <div class="commentary">The people are altered by their prince and thereby become greedy. They rebel against Tao and are opposed to Te. Therefore they are hungry.</div>

>> <div class="note">After 貪 "greedy" one edition has the final particle 矣.</div>

The people's being difficult to govern comes from their prince's fussing about.

> <div class="commentary">That the people cannot be governed has its reason in their prince's having many wishes and wanting to fuss about.</div>

Therefore they are difficult to govern.

> <div class="commentary">The people are altered by their prince and fuss about. Their character becomes hypocritical, and they are difficult to govern.</div>

>> <div class="note">The Tao-tsang edition begins the first sentence with 是以 "thereby" and omits the final particle 也 at the end of the second sentence.</div>

That men take death easy comes from their seeking for the fulness of life.

> <div class="commentary">That the people easily risk their lives is because their way of seeking for life is too much bent on gain. Thereby they endanger themselves.</div>

>> <div class="note">Instead of 道 "way" v.1. 事 "doings".</div>

Therefore they take death easy.

> <div class="commentary">Because they seek for the great fulness of life, they take it easy to get themselves on death-bringing ground.</div>

>> <div class="note">The expression 死地 se-ti, here translated by "death-bringing ground" occurs in ch. 50 where it has a different meaning, indicating a deadly spot of the body. Here it is doubtless to be taken in another sense, probably as a military expression found in Sun-tse II, Ia, where se-ti occurs among the nine strategically important formations of the ground and is defined as follows: "Where one survives by fighting quickly and perishes without fighting quickly, there is the death-bringing ground". Lionel Giles, Sun-tse (1910), p. 117, translates "desparate ground". Waley, The Way and Its Power (1934), seems in his commentary on ch. 50 to have mistaken Ho-shang-kung's explanation for that of Sun-tse, as he correctly translates se-ti by "death-spot" but explains it as "a military expression" which it is not in this sense.</div>

Now he who lays stress on life is worthier that he who values life.

> <div class="commentary">If one now thinks life alone not the most important thing, if dignities and emoluments are not occupying the mind, if wealth and profit do not enter the thoughts, if the emperor does not get you for his minister and the prince does not get you for his official, this is wiser than valuing life.</div>

>> <div class="note">Instead of 干 "enter" v.1. 上, making no sense and therefore to be rejected. Instead of 心 "mind" v.1. 身 "body", to be rejected for the same reason, as Li Ch'iao remarks. The sentence "If the emperor does not get you for his minister and the prince does not get you for his official" is a quotation from Chuang-tse 28, 7b (SBE 40, 158), apart from the last word which in Chuang-tse is 友 "friend" instead of 使 "official", Li Ch'iao quotes an unintelligible variant 有, probably a misprint for 友. For Pelliot's remarks on this passage, see introduction, note 8.</div>
