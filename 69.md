# Ch. 69 How to use the dark one.

For the use of weapons there is a saying:

> <div class="commentary">This represents the principle of the use of arms. Lao-tse loathed the militarism of his time. Therefore he himself relies on its ideas.</div>

>> <div class="note">The first sentence 陳用兵之道 is not clear. It might also be translated: "This is a saying of the regular troops." The meaning of the last sentence is: "He wants to refute the militarists by their own arguments."</div>

I dare not play the host

> <div class="commentary">The host goes ahead. He dares not take up arms first.</div>

>> <div class="note">Instead of 舉兵 "take up arms" v.1. 興 | "raise troops".</div>

but I play the guest.

> <div class="commentary">The guest adapts himself and does not take the lead. The warrior must conform to heaven and [only] move afterwards.</div>

I dare not advance an inch but I retire a foot.

> <div class="commentary">He encroaches upon the positions of others, he uses the riches of others, he advances and closes the gates, he holds the city and retires.</div>

This is called to go without going,

> <div class="commentary">Such following without stopping makes him the empire's thief. Though it is possible to punish him, it is impossible to seize him.</div>

>> <div class="note">Instead of 賊 "thief" v.1. 賤 "inferior", which makes no sense. Instead of 執 "seize" v.1. 誅 "punish", which is equally without sense.</div>

to stretch without arms,

> <div class="commentary">Though he wants to stretch his arms, he is as angry as if he had no arms to stretch.</div>

>> <div class="note">The characters 攘臂 "to stretch his arms" are missing in one edition.</div>

to seize without arms,

> <div class="commentary">Though he would like to seize something, it is as if he were handling and using a weapon without a blade. How could he hurt them and enmesh the people into sin before heaven? If this happens to a prince who has no Tao, he will not bear to lose it.</div>

to attract without an enemy.

> <div class="commentary">Though he would like to go and attract the hearts, it is as if he could attract them without having an enemy.</div>

>> <div class="note">行 "to go" is wanting in one edition.</div>

No misfortune is greater than making light of the enemy.

> <div class="commentary">Now no unfortunate confusion is greater than a deceptive underestimation of the enemy. By incessantly grasping for an underrated enemy one will lose.</div>

If one makes light of the enemy, one is near losing the jewel of the ego.

> <div class="commentary">幾 = near. The jewel is the body. By deceptive underrating of the enemy one comes near to the loss of the body.</div>

Therefore when opposed warriors measure their strength,

> <div class="commentary">Two enemies at war.</div>

then the compassionate one will be victorious.

> <div class="commentary">The compassionate one is the benign and humane warrior. He does not come near to death.</div>

>> <div class="note">Li Ch'iao doubts the correctness of the text. He quotes a variant of the last sentence: "Surely he will keep aloof from underrating the enemy." The Tao-tsang edition reads: He does not keep it away from his self.</div>
