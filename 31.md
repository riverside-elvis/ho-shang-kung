# Ch. 31 How to suppress war.

Now beautiful weapons are tools of evil.

> <div class="commentary">佳 (beautiful) = ornamented. 祥 = good. Weapons excite the spirit and dim the harmonious atmosphere. They are evil tools. One ought not to use them [even] if they are ornamented.</div>

>> <div class="note">The explanation 佳飾也 is missing in most editions, which might point to the character 佳 as also having been absent from the original text of Lao-tse. For several other reasons which may be adduced for this assumption, see Erkes in Artibus Asiae V, 306/7.</div>

Each of the creatures hates them.

> <div class="commentary">If weapons are set in motion, somebody is hurt. Therefore there is no creature that would not hate them.</div>

Therefore he who has Tao does not remain with them.

> <div class="commentary">A man possessing Tao does not stay in such a country.</div>

The superior man stays at home and honours the left side.

> <div class="commentary">He honours what is tender and weak.</div>

Who makes use of weapons honours the right side.

> <div class="commentary">He honours what is hard and strong. This means that the way of the warrior and the way of the superior man are opposed to each other. What they honour is different.</div>

>> <div class="note">As Ho-shang-kung thinks of the superior man and of the man who uses weapons as of two different persons, the translation of the text must conform to his conception.</div>

Weapons are the tools of evil.

> <div class="commentary">Weapons and cuirasses are no good tools.</div>

They are not tools of the superior man.

> <div class="commentary">They are not tools that are esteemed and thought to be important by a superior man.</div>

If he cannot act otherwise, then he uses them.

> <div class="commentary">This means: If he suffers from weakness and confusion, if the misery is going to spread to the people, then he uses them.</div>

To be peaceful and quiet makes a prince.

> <div class="commentary">He craves not for the land of the nobles nor for the people's riches.</div>

>> <div class="note">The curious expression 士地 which would mean "the land of the nobles" or "of the officials" is perhaps only a misprint for the current 土地 "territories". By some mistake, the Tao-tsang edition does not contain this part of the commentary but prints the next explanation twice.</div>

If he is victorious, he does not think it beautiful.

> <div class="commentary">Though he wins a victory, he does not think it a blessing.</div>

But who thinks this beautiful enjoys the killing of men.

> <div class="commentary">To think it beautiful to win a victory means to enjoy the killing of men.</div>

Now who enjoys the killing of men may not realise his intentions on the empire.

> <div class="commentary">Who, being a prince, enjoys the killing of men is unable to practise his intentions on the empire. Who, being a prince, is bent on forcing the destiny of men, wrongly deals out punishments.</div>

>> <div class="note">Instead of 誅 "punishments" v.1. 罰 same meaning.</div>

Therefore for happy occasions one chooses the left.

> <div class="commentary">The left side is the throne of life.</div>

For unhappy occasions one chooses the right.

> <div class="commentary">The Yin-Tao is deadly.</div>

>> <div class="note">Various readings are "the Yin-Tao kills men" and "the right is the Yin-Tao".</div>

The general sits to the left.

> <div class="commentary">The general is a subordinate but remains in the Yang, as killing is not his main business.</div>

The marshal sits to the right.

> <div class="commentary">The marshal is more prominent but he sits to the right, as killing is his main business.</div>

This means: according to the rites of mourning he sits there.

> <div class="commentary">The marshal sits to the right. For the rites of mourning the right side is chosen, as the dead honour the Yin.</div>

The killed multitudes are wept for according to the rites of mourning.

> <div class="commentary">If one hurts the self, Tao diminishes. If one is not able to change men by Tao, one hurts the harmless people.</div>

The victor in battle ought to sit there according to the rites of mourning.

> <div class="commentary">In ancient times, the general who had won a battle sat according to the rites of mourning on the seat of honour, wearing white robes and weeping. This meant that a superior man honours Te and despises the weapons. If he is unable to eradicate misery, his heart is sorry as if he were in mourning. He knows that posterity will use the weapons without accomplishing anything. Therefore he suffers.</div>
