# Ch. 81 How to display reality.

Sincere words are not beautiful.

> <div class="commentary">Sincere words are true words. What is not beautiful is simple and real.</div>

>> <div class="note">The first sentence has the following variant 信言者如其實 (the Tao-tsang edition adds 也) "Sincere words correspond to their reality".</div>

Beautiful words are not sincere.

> <div class="commentary">Beautiful words are superficial talks of rich beauty. Insincerity is outward, hypocritical and extremely empty.</div>

>> <div class="note">For the first sentence, Li Ch'iao gives the following variant: 道美之言者孳孳華詞 "Superficial and beautiful words are incessant flowery talks." The Tao-tsang edition has the same reading but instead of the last two characters 美辭 "beautiful sayings".</div>

The knowing one is no scholar.

> <div class="commentary">The knowing one is the knowing Taoist. The unlearned one comprehends unity as the origin.</div>

>> <div class="note">A variant of the last sentence reads: 知道守一則不必博 "Who knows Tao and preserves unity is surely no scholar".</div>

The scholar is ignorant.

> <div class="commentary">The scholar sees and hears much, but as he is ignorant, be loses what is important and true.</div>

>> <div class="note">V.1.: 多見聞失眞要. 故不知. "As he sees and hears much, he loses what is most important. Therefore he is ignorant."</div>

The good man does not discuss.

> <div class="commentary">The good man practises asceticism by means of Tao. As he does not cultivate discussions, he has no outward elegance.</div>

Who discusses is not good.

> <div class="commentary">Who discusses speaks beautiful words. As he is not good, he creates sorrow by means of his tongue. If a mountain contains jade, that mountain will be dug out. If a lake contains pearls, its depths will be sullied. If a discussing mouth speaks much, it will lose its body.</div>

>> <div class="note">Li Ch'iao notes that a T'ang edition writes 泉 ch'üan "source" instead of 淵 yüan "depth", as yüan was a tabooed character under the T'ang, being the personal name of the emperor T'ang Kao-tsu (Li Yüan 李淵).</div>

A saint does not hoard.

> <div class="commentary">A saint hoards Te but he does not hoard treasures. If he has Te, he instructs the stupid ones. If he possesses treasures, he gives them to the poor.</div>

>> <div class="note">Instead of 與 "gives" v.1. 施 "presents".</div>

Having worked for others he has himself the more.

> <div class="commentary">After having divided his Te among others, he himself has got the more of it.</div>

>> <div class="note">This commentary is missing in one edition, as Li Ch'iao remarks.</div>

Having given to others he himself possesses the more.

> <div class="commentary">After having divided his treasures among others, his own treasures have augmented the more, as the splendour of the sun and moon never becomes exhausted.</div>

>> <div class="note">Instead of 施於人 "divided among others" v.1. | 與 |, the same.</div>

The way of heaven is to bless and not to hurt.

> <div class="commentary">Heaven begets all beings, loves them and educates them, makes them grow and never hurts them.</div>

The way of the saint is to act and not to contend.

> <div class="commentary">The saint takes heaven for his model. He displays nothing but perfects his affairs. Then he does not contend with his subjects. His merit becomes famous, and therefore he is able to complete his saintly merit.</div>
