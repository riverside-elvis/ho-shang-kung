# Ch. 56 On mysterious Te.

The knowing one does not talk.

> <div class="commentary">The knowing one esteems deeds and not words.</div>

>> <div class="note">Instead of "deeds" v.1. "his doings". The Tao-tsang edition reads: "The knowing one esteems walking in Tao; he does not esteem words."</div>

The talker knows nothing.

> <div class="commentary">A quadriga does not catch the tongue. Who talks much, will have much sorrow.</div>

>> <div class="note">The Tao-tsang edition inverts the sequence of the sentences. The sentence "A quadriga does not catch the tongue" is a quotation from Lun-yü 12, 8.</div>

Stop up these openings, bar this gate.

> <div class="commentary">Who stops them up and bars them, wants to cut off his sources.</div>

Blunt this sharpness.

> <div class="commentary">Feelings and desires involve sharp action. One must think of Tao and non-action. Thereby they are blunted and stopped.</div>

>> <div class="note">Comp. the entirely different explanations given of this sentence and the three following ones in ch. 4 which also necessitate a partially different translation of the accompanying text of Lao-tse.</div>

Sever this connection.

> <div class="commentary">If the connection with hatred does not cease, one must think of Tao and non-action. Thereby it will be severed.</div>

>> <div class="note">Instead of 無爲 "non-action" v.1. 淡泊 "quietness".</div>

Harmonize this splendour.

> <div class="commentary">Though you may possess the enlightenment of unique insight, you must harmonize it. Let it become obscured, do not let it radiate.</div>

Become one with this dust.

> <div class="commentary">You ought not to keep aloof.</div>

This is called the union with the dark one.

> <div class="commentary">The dark one is heaven. If man is able to do these superior deeds, this means that he together with heaven will become united to Tao.</div>

>> <div class="note">On this explanation, to which Kao Yu alludes in his commentary on Huai-nan-tse 16, 7b, comp. the introduction and note on ch. 1.</div>

Therefore one is not able to obtain inclination.

> <div class="commentary">One ought not to enjoy glory nor to suffer from loneliness.</div>

Nor is one able to obtain disinclination.

> <div class="commentary">The will is quiet and without desire or aversion.</div>

One does not obtain bliss.

> <div class="commentary">The body does not long for wealth and honour, the mouth does not long for the five tastes.</div>

Nor does one obtain calamity.

> <div class="commentary">One does not avidly strive for gain. One does not bravely contend for breath.</div>

One does not obtain honour.

> <div class="commentary">One does not confuse the master of the world. One does not stay near the throne of a benighted prince.</div>

Nor does one obtain disgrace.

> <div class="commentary">One does not become haughty through power obtained. One is not depressed through failing intentions.</div>

Therefore one is esteemed within the empire.

> <div class="commentary">Him whose Te is of this kind the emperor will not get for his minister, and the princes will not succeed in humiliating him. He sinks and swims together with the world, he makes his body light and avoids calamity. Therefore he is esteemed within the empire.</div>

>> <div class="note">In the last phrase "within the empire" the Tao-tsang edition writes t'ien-hsia che kuei 天下之貴 instead of t'ien-hsia kuei.</div>
